How Local Farms Are Adapting to Climate Change

By Sarah Park · January 20, 2026

In the sun-scorched fields east of the valley, third-generation farmer Tom Reyes is trying something his grandfather never imagined: growing high-altitude coffee varieties at sea level.

"The frost dates have shifted three weeks in my lifetime," Reyes said, walking between rows of experimental Gesha plants. "We either adapt or we sell the land."

According to county agricultural data, average growing-season temperatures in the region have risen 2.1°F since 1990. The shift has made traditional crops less viable while opening possibilities for subtropical varieties previously confined to greenhouse cultivation.

Dr. Elena Vasquez, a climate adaptation researcher at the state university, called the local shift "a preview of what's coming nationwide."
